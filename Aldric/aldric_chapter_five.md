# Aldric — Chapter Five
## A Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Prologue

*Ten days collapse into their shape.*

*You ride back to Deepstone with Kael. Edwick is still on the floor with the records when you arrive, surrounded by six hundred years of numbers and the specific focused energy of a man who has found the most important data he will ever read and is not going to stop until he understands all of it.*

*You tell him about the chamber. About the figure. About the answer.*

*He listens without writing — which tells you everything about how significant it is, because Edwick always writes.*

---

## Part One: Deepstone Days

Six days at Deepstone before Sera's party rides to the mountain halls.

Edwick writes. Kael prepares testimony. Sera organizes the expedition with the efficiency of a clan-master who has been managing frontier logistics for thirty years. Fera checks the knee every morning and reports steady improvement.

On the third day, a rider arrives from the coast. From Lenne.

Three pages — the account she promised, everything from the canal in Thornwall to the beginning of Chapter Three. Clear. Complete. Unsparing about the difficult moments and direct about the ones that mattered.

The postscript:

*The second leak in Saltmere has been identified. His name is Aldric Hews — no relation to the commander. A clerk in the garrison's supply office, providing Verath with shipment manifests for two years. Commander Hews arrested him four days ago following an anonymous tip. The tip came from Dara Voss.*

*Voss's driver was found in the hills northeast of Saltmere, alive. He had been held at an abandoned farmstead for eleven days. He is recovering.*

*My father sends his regards. He has read Edwick's account three times.*

*One more thing. A goblin clan has occupied the Ashford palisade. They arrived six days ago. The Rememberer's twenty warriors are camped outside the east wall. The emerged beings are in the depression. The goblins are inside the palisade.*

*They have not left. No one has asked them to leave. The warriors report that the goblins appear to be watching the beings. With significant interest.*

*— L*

You read the goblin paragraph twice. Hand it to Edwick without comment.

> *"The goblins and the orcs were the original inhabitants of the plains and forests,"* he says. *"Both of them. Before the elves, before humans. They were here when the beings were first caged."* A pause. *"What do the goblins remember."*

You write to the Rememberer: don't approach the goblins, don't ask them to leave, watch what they watch, receive contact if it's initiated, report anything significant.

Then you turn back to the work in front of you.

On the fifth day, Root's letter arrives from the coastal estate. Three pages in a hand so precise it looks like engraving.

*The Lord Commander is a remarkable man. He has asked me eleven hundred and forty-three questions in six days. I have answered all of them.*

*The eastern range clan-council — the Irongate clan — has agreed to attend the elder-council as an observer. The eastern range has its own records. They have been sharing them with no one for two hundred years.*

*— Root*

*P.S. The Lord Commander has a daughter. She asks about you. I have told her you are functional. She seemed to find this insufficient.*

## Part Two: The Mountain Halls

Four days from Deepstone, climbing steadily. The plains fall away. The air thins. The enchanted stone in the rock faces carries the same warm presence as the chamber — constant, structural, the mountain's own memory held in the materials of its making.

On the second day, the first of the great carved gates. Stonework centuries old that looks finished yesterday because enchanted stone holds its state against time's erosion.

On the third day, descent into the mountain itself.

The deep halls open with the grandeur of accumulated depth — not towers and facades but the feeling of being inside something very large that has been making itself larger for a very long time. Fire in iron brackets. Carved walls. The sound of work in distant chambers.

And everywhere: measuring instruments. In alcoves, on workbenches, mounted in carved niches at regular intervals throughout the main corridor. Reading the network. Recording. Automatically. The work of four hundred years of craft that always knew what it was doing and never knew why.

Kael walks beside you looking at every instrument they pass.

> *"I grew up here. I passed these every day. I thought they were decorative."*
>
> *"You thought they were decoration and you still felt the need to find your own and make your own records for twelve years."*
>
> *"Yes."*
>
> *"The craft knew. You felt it before you understood it."*

The council chamber is carved from a single vast rock face at the deepest accessible level. Circular. High ceiling. Acoustics that carry every word to every part of the room.

Fifteen elders. Thirteen from the western range clans, two from the eastern. The Irongate representative arrived two hours early and has been in private conversation with a second eastern dwarf. She watches the room with the attention of someone who has been waiting for this meeting for a long time.

Sera reads the elders' faces.

> *"The two Irongate representatives — the one who's been here two hours, she's not an observer. She's a senior council member. The Irongate clan sent someone with actual authority."*
>
> *"They already know,"* you say. *"The answer. Or enough of it."*

---

## Part Three: The Opening

You stand. You use everything you have.

Not the words first. The room first. Fifteen faces read as terrain — two openly resistant, three visibly curious, one watching you with the specific attention of someone who knows what you're about to say and wants to see how you say it.

The Irongate senior council member nods, barely. The nod of someone passing an acknowledgment.

*The Go-Between — +4 Persuasion.*

> *"I am going to tell you something that your craft has known for four hundred years. Your minds don't know it yet. But your hands do."*

You tell them what the network is. What it contained. What the renewal did. What the door has opened. What the figure in the chamber said. What the dwarfs were built for.

Not as an accusation. As a fact delivered with the respect that significant truths deserve — said plainly, without apology or diminishment.

> *"The network built you. Your craft, your deep knowledge, your unconstructed levels that the elder-council has officially described as geologically unstable — all of it was preparation. The specific places inside the mountains that will be needed when the surface of the plains returns to the beings who were there before anyone else."*

You pause.

> *"I am not asking you to decide today whether to give up the mountains. The answer the world's memory offered is not a displacement. It is an expansion. The mountain halls become larger — large enough for every people currently on the plains, across generations, planned and negotiated and built by the people who were specifically prepared to build it. The question is not whether you want this. The question is whether you were built for it."* A pause. *"The answer is yes. The question remaining is whether you will choose to be what you were built to be."*

The Irongate senior council member stands.

> *"The eastern range has maintained records for two hundred years that we have not shared with the western range clans. We did not share them because we did not understand what they meant and we were afraid of what they implied. We share them now."*

She opens the case at her feet.

Architectural surveys. Two hundred years of unconstructed level mapping in the eastern range. Level after level, descending further than any official record acknowledges, the halls already formed, waiting with the patience of enchanted stone.

Edwick, quietly:

> *"The current total population of all peoples on the central plains is approximately eighty thousand. The combined unconstructed capacity of both ranges — existing infrastructure, before any active expansion — is one hundred and twenty thousand. Minimum."*

> *"There is room,"* you say. *"Not metaphorically. Architecturally. Specifically. Right now."*

---

## Part Four: The Testimony

You step back. Kael steps forward.

They speak in Khazdul first — the account of a researcher speaking to their community about work done in private. Ten minutes. The council listens with the quality of attention that dwarfs bring to someone from within their own tradition.

Then Kael switches to the Common Tongue.

> *"I want the hunter to hear this part. Because he is the one who sat in the hollow on the shelf and waited for me to come to him rather than demanding I explain myself, and that is why I'm standing here."*

They tell the story of Maret Deepstone, who found the channel fifty years ago and was afraid to report anomalous readings she couldn't explain, and chose to hold the contradiction between what the elder-council said and what her instruments showed rather than abandoning either.

> *"The craft knew. Maret's hands knew. Vor's hands knew. My hands know. We made the instruments and took the readings and kept the records because the craft required it and the craft was older and more certain than our understanding. I am asking the council to accept that the craft knows things our minds haven't caught up with yet. That is, I believe, a dwarven concept that this council is familiar with."*

The resistant elder raises an objection. Kael translates: if the network built them to build the halls, then they are a tool — used without consent.

Kael answers.

> *"A hammer does not choose to be a hammer. But a master craftsperson does not use a hammer without understanding what it was made for. The network gave us the capacity. We chose to use it. Maret chose to keep measuring when she could have stopped. Vor chose to continue when Maret died. I chose to go back to the shelf eight days after my last visit because I felt something had changed. The network gave us the capacity. We chose to use it."* A pause. *"That is not being a tool. That is being a people who know what they're for and choose to be it."*

The resistant elder stops speaking.

---

## Part Five: The Question

The Irongate senior council member has one question for you before deliberation.

> *"If the sequence was so carefully designed, why did it need a Go-Between? Why did it need a human hunter with a survey commission and a damaged knee?"*

You answer.

> *"Because the pieces are living things. The sequence was designed. The socket was built for the elves. The dwarfs were built for the halls. The Rememberers preserved the memory. The key was placed four days before I found it. But none of that produced the outcome by itself. Root had to choose to leave the grove. The Rememberer had to choose to come south. The Lord Commander had to choose to hold the army. Edwick had to choose to rewrite the conclusion on the boat. Kael had to choose to keep measuring for twelve years when no one asked them to."*

You look at the room.

> *"The world's memory designed the sequence. It couldn't design the choices. Those had to come from the pieces themselves — from living things making decisions in circumstances where other decisions were available. A Go-Between doesn't force agreement. They create the conditions where agreement becomes possible and then they wait to see if the living things choose it. I didn't bring you the answer. The answer was already in the mountains and in the oral tradition and in fifty years of unauthorized research and two hundred years of unconstructed levels that nobody officially acknowledged. I just walked between them until they could hear each other."*

The Irongate member sits.

---

## Part Six: The Deliberation

Four hours.

Dwarven deliberation: structured, sequential, each elder speaking in turn with the specific contribution of their clan's perspective. Building toward a position the way an arch is built — each stone dependent on the ones beside it, stable only when complete.

At the end of the second hour, Kael:

> *"The resistant elder has changed position. She wants to know the timeline. How long before the beings resume. Whether the migration can be voluntary and generational rather than forced and immediate. Those are practical questions. She's moved from whether to how."*

At the end of the third hour, the Irongate member speaks for twenty minutes. The eastern range numbers. Cost in labor, time in years, the phasing of which levels to open first.

> *"She's been preparing this presentation for two hundred years,"* Kael murmurs.

The fourth hour is quieter. The elders have said what they came to say. Now they look at the records on the floor — fifty years of instrument readings beside two hundred years of architectural surveys.

Sera calls the vote.

Fifteen elders.

The marks accumulate.

> *"Thirteen to two,"* Sera says, in the Common Tongue. *"The council authorizes the expansion survey. The unconstructed levels will be officially mapped and assessed within six months. A formal proposal for the migration framework will be drafted and presented to the human and orc governing bodies within one year. The two dissenting elders have requested that the migration be explicitly voluntary and generational, which it will be."*

Edwick, quietly:

> *"Thirteen to two."*

With the specific quality of a man who has been doing arithmetic in margins for weeks and has just watched the most important sum of his life balance correctly.

---

## Epilogue: The Longer Work

The Irongate senior council member crosses the room and extends her hand.

> *"Two hundred years. My predecessors kept those records for two hundred years and told no one because they were afraid of what they meant. How did you know to wait for me to share them rather than demanding I explain myself?"*
>
> *"You came with a case at your feet that you'd had since before the meeting started. That meant you'd made your decision before you arrived. Whatever you were waiting for, you were ready to go when it came. I just made sure the thing you were waiting for arrived."*
>
> *"You are very good at this,"* she says.
>
> *"I'm getting better,"* you say.

That evening, three letters.

To **Root**: the vote, the timeline, the authorization. Stay with the Lord Commander. The army's held status needs to become permanent policy while the framework is built.

To **the Rememberer**: the vote confirmed. When the goblins are ready to speak, the framework needs to include them.

To **Lenne**:

*The council voted thirteen to two. The answer is yes. There is room.*

*The work ahead is enormous. The migration framework, the expansion survey, the human and orc governing bodies, the goblins, the surface-dweller agreement that will take a generation to complete. None of it is done. But the question has been answered and the people who need to know it are beginning to know it.*

*Your father's formal cooperation is needed. I am asking you to be the one who carries this to the Lord Commander — not as his daughter but as a witness to everything that happened and someone who understands why it matters.*

*I know that's a significant ask.*

*You'll do it anyway.*

*— A*

You sit in the guest quarters of the mountain hall with the knee elevated and the bow in reach and the sounds of a people who have been building specific places for centuries without knowing who for, and you think about what comes next.

The migration framework. The human governing bodies. The orcs' formal agreement. The goblins. Fifty years, a hundred years, the generational work of moving every people currently on the central plains into the mountains in an arrangement that is voluntary and sustainable and fair to everyone — including the beings who have been waiting ten thousand years to come home.

You have found the answer.

Now comes the longer work of making it real.

*End of Chapter Five*
