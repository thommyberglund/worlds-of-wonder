# Aldric — Chapter Two
## A Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Prologue

*The road south runs two days to Saltmere. You ride it with Lenne and the memory of nothing useful, which turns out to be its own kind of freedom.*

---

## Part One: The Overturned Cart

The first day passes clean. Plains, sky, the sea appearing and disappearing to the west between low headlands. Two refugee columns moving north — families with carts, a few soldiers among them who look at your bow and your quiver and your companion's awl and don't ask questions. Lenne watches the columns with the expression of someone counting and finding the numbers insufficient.

The second day brings rain. Cold, horizontal, the coastal kind that finds the gaps in your collar and sets up residence.

An hour from Saltmere you find the cart.

Overturned in a ditch, one wheel still spinning in the rain. No horse — the traces are cut, not broken, which means someone cut them deliberately and took the animal. The cart's contents scattered in the mud: bolts of cloth, a merchant's strongbox lying open and empty, papers going to pulp in the rain.

One person. Sitting in the ditch with her back against the overturned cart, knees drawn up, a cut above her eyebrow leaking pink in the rain. Forty, heavy merchant's coat, the particular stillness of someone who has finished being frightened and arrived at numb.

> *"They took my horse,"* she says. *"And my money. And my driver. He didn't go willingly."*

The scene reads fast. The traces cut clean — one stroke of a good blade. Professional spacing in the boot prints around the cart — four, maybe five people. The strongbox opened with a key, not forced. Horses northeast, along a drainage channel. A fire ring sixty meters off the road, two days old at least. Pre-positioned. This wasn't opportunistic. They were waiting.

Her name is **Dara Voss**. She was carrying correspondence for three parties — two trade letters and a sealed packet with black wax and no mark, to be delivered to the harbormaster at Saltmere. The man who gave it to her was wearing red and grey.

Lenne gets her onto the horse. You ride for Saltmere.

---

## Part Two: Saltmere

**Commander Aldric Hews** reads Undh's letter standing up. He looks at you. At Lenne. At Voss with her head wound and borrowed horse.

> *"Undh says you're reliable."* Not a compliment. More like a property of a tool being confirmed before use.

You tell him about the cart, the pre-positioned camp, the red and grey livery, the letter addressed to his harbormaster. He accepts this with the controlled frustration of a man who runs a city and has just discovered it runs partly on information he wasn't receiving.

> *"Cael Dross. My harbormaster."* He says it the way a man says the name of a thing he suspected and didn't want confirmed. *"He's been expecting a delivery from Thornwall for four days. He hasn't mentioned it to me."*

Dross arrives ten minutes later. You read him in the two seconds before anyone speaks — his eyes go to Voss's coat, not her face. Recognition of an expected delivery failing to materialize. His weight on his back foot. A thickening in his left breast pocket. Paper. Instructions he was ready to act on.

> *"The letter didn't arrive,"* you say. *"The cart was taken on the road. Your driver—"* you watch his left eye— *"is in the hills northeast with the people who took it."*

The left eye twitches. Not Voss's driver. *His* driver. He put someone on that cart.

Hews blocks the door. Dross sits down.

> *"I want a guarantee. Before I say anything. A written guarantee from the Lord Commander."*
>
> *"The Lord Commander is two days north,"* Hews says.
>
> *"Then I'll wait."*
>
> *"You'll talk now,"* you say, *"or I'll go into those hills and find whoever you sent with that cart, and whatever they tell me won't have your name attached to a guarantee."*

Dross talks.

Eight months ago a Verath factor named Edwick approached him. Academic questions at first — harbor access, historical records, depth soundings. Then, three months later, money. Private mooring for the fast vessel. No cargo inspection. Departure without logging. Notification of garrison inquiries. He accepted. He warned them. He is, in the final accounting, a man who made a series of small decisions that compounded into something he is now sitting in a room with.

The document in his pocket is in cipher. You can read a third of it through Edwick's habit of slipping into plain script mid-sentence. A departure time — third hour after midnight, two days hence — and a destination rendered in cipher except for one word:

*Greyvane.*

Where the army is mustering. Where an army commander waits for a letter from the Lord Commander that never arrived because the messenger ended up in a troll's stomach.

Hews isolates Dross. You begin planning.

---

## Part Three: The Harbor

The fast Verath vessel is berthed in the old harbor — the silted crescent behind the working harbor, a stone quay unused for forty years. Low in the water. Whatever they loaded, they loaded at night.

You go aboard at midnight. Stern rope, quay, the unlatched gate Dross arranged. Clean over the rail, Lenne two seconds behind.

Below decks: breathing. Five or six distinct patterns in the hold. Not cargo. People.

You find Edwick's cabin from the candlelight under his door. You knock three times, evenly spaced. The scratching of his quill stops.

> *"It's not locked,"* he says.

He is older-looking than he was in the tower three days ago. The candle is low. He has been on this boat a long time by the measure of the wax. He looks at you. At Lenne. Makes the calculation of relief and dread arriving simultaneously.

Three Verath scholars, a surveyor, and two soldiers are in the hold. They are sailing to Greyvane to brief the army commanders before any official communication from Thornwall arrives. The briefing: the Warden is loose, degrading, controllable. Verath has the means. An army of the dead could clear the central plains in a single campaign season.

Edwick has been rewriting the conclusion for three days.

The revision keeps the forty years of evidence intact — every expedition record, every binding document, every technical detail of the hollow point surveys, because it is accurate and removing it would look tampered. What he has changed is the conclusion and the recommendation.

New conclusion: the binding was renewed by the alternative method at the hollow point beneath Thornwall's trading hall by a living authority acting without coercion. The Warden is contained for a minimum of one thousand years.

New recommendation: House Verath's research should be formally archived. Their private military force should stand down. Their scholar delegation should be received as witnesses to the historical record, not strategic advisors.

It is the most elegant thing you have seen done with ink.

> *"You need a witness,"* Lenne says. *"Someone who was present at the renewal."*

> *"We need to be on this boat when it sails,"* you say.

---

## Part Four: The Dockworker

You get off the boat and go to Hews. Provisions for four days. A sealed letter for the Greyvane garrison commander. Ten marks. A waxed canvas cover for the bow.

Then Dross tries to warn his contact from his cell. The guard reports it. There is a second leak — someone above Dross who trusted him with the departure schedule and hasn't yet been identified.

You spend the night before departure hunting the second leak through the harbor district. At false dawn, cold and empty-handed, you stop thinking about the leak and start thinking about the chain of information — and two Verath soldiers in red and grey close the gap behind you before you finish the thought.

You run. Left into a chandler's alley, too narrow for two abreast. Across a service quay at a stride longer than comfortable. Up the harbormaster's walkway and over the side into the working harbor's service lane before the second soldier cuts you off. You walk three streets with professional calm and your collar torn and your hip starting to bruise and a Verath network that has now confirmed your face.

Hews gives you a dockworker's coat. An oil-stained cap. Lenne tells you to leave the bow.

You leave the bow.

The working harbor at dawn. A fish stall. A Verath soldier twenty meters away running the comparison — coat, cap, absent bow, a dockworker haggling over mackerel.

> *"The eyes on this one are cloudy."*
>
> *"Caught this morning."*
>
> *"Caught this morning and already cloudy."*

The soldier's footsteps resume their previous pace. Negative identification. You buy two mackerel, tuck them under your arm, and walk to the old harbor gate.

Edwick looks at the fish when you come through his cabin door.

> *"I got hungry on the way back,"* you say.

He finds a knife in the desk drawer and makes breakfast.

---

## Part Five: The Voyage

Third hour after midnight. The vessel moves.

Saltmere's walls fall away, then the headland, then there is only the dark sea and the three moons hanging cold and bright above the water.

You sleep. Deeply. The sleep of a body that has been spending resources faster than it has been replenishing them and has finally been given permission to collect the debt.

The first day passes in rest and quiet. Edwick works. Lenne reads his papers and asks questions you half-hear from the cot. The Verath scholars forward move at mealtimes with the cadence of academic argument — people comfortable with each other who disagree habitually.

On the second morning you sit up and feel like yourself.

Greyvane appears on the middle distance — a city on a flat headland, lower than Thornwall, wider. The harbor bristling with masts in a quantity that makes Saltmere look like a fishing village. Hundreds of vessels. Thousands of soldiers.

You get off the boat before the Verath passengers have finished gathering their materials. Clean, fast, moving with harbor traffic before they reach the gangplank. Then a Verath soldier on the vessel's deck sees you. Not clearly. Not certainly. But watching, cataloguing departures.

Minutes. You have minutes.

> *"Lenne."* She's already looking. *"Take Edwick to the garrison. Show them Hews's letter. Get in front of whoever commands this muster and don't leave until they've agreed to hear you. Tell them a hunter from Thornwall is coming with a witness and a document that contradicts the Verath briefing. Use my name."*

You hand her the letter and turn back to the harbor.

---

## Part Six: The Briefing

You find the garrison dock agent before the scholars reach the gangplank — young, harried, managing a hundred vessel arrivals a day. You tell him the Verath vessel is carrying undeclared cargo and that Commander Hews of Saltmere has requested a full inspection. You mention Hews's name twice. You do not mention your own.

He goes. He does stop the scholars. It holds them seven minutes instead of three.

You walk to the garrison.

---

**Marshal Yeva Cross** is sixty, lean, with close-cropped grey hair and the eyes of someone who has been lied to by clever people often enough to develop a specific instrument for detecting it. She is reading Edwick's document when you enter. She finishes the page before she looks up.

Lenne got here first. Edwick's document went in five minutes ago. The campaign marshal is being summoned.

You tell her what you came to tell her. She listens. Then:

> *"Why should I find your version more credible than theirs?"*

You look at her steadily.

> *"You shouldn't. Not on the basis of anything I've told you."*

You take her through it — not the argument that you're right, but the argument that the symmetry between your account and Verath's can be broken. A rider to Thornwall. Four days there, four days back. The army moves in three weeks. The time exists if she chooses to spend it.

> *"Verath's delegation has been here four days and hasn't suggested you verify anything. Ask yourself why."*

Cross dispatches the rider. She does not authorize Verath's proposal. She does not dismiss it. She invites you to be present, silent, in the corner when the Verath delegation arrives within the hour.

---

The lead scholar is named **Maret** — a different Maret, a common name, but the half-second of dislocation lands anyway. She sees you when she enters and her composure holds with professional impressiveness. Whatever Verath's Saltmere network passed forward, she knew you were here. The advantage of surprise is gone before the meeting begins.

She presents for thirty minutes. She has pre-emptively answered three of your four arguments. The fourth — *why hasn't Verath suggested you verify anything* — she doesn't address.

Then you notice the surveyor.

His name is **Davin Ost**. Forty, weathered by open country, the hands of a man who has unrolled ten thousand maps. He holds the survey map flat on the campaign table and his finger rests on a hollow point marker in the frontier zone.

X=520, Y=700.

Ashford.

You speak.

> *"That marker. X=520, Y=700. What did you find there."*

The room recalibrates. Scholar Maret's composure holds. The soldier at the door shifts weight. And Ost — Ost lifts his finger from the map and looks at you with the recognition of a man who has been to a place looking at someone else who has also been to that place.

> *"A village,"* he says. *"Or what was left of one."*

Maret tries to redirect. Cross says: *"Let him answer."*

Eight months ago Verath surveyed the Ashford site. They found a soft place — a location where the boundary between the living world and what lies beneath runs thin. Elevated permeability during the period of binding degradation. Enough that Ost noted it in the survey record as requiring monitoring.

> *"Did Verath monitor it,"* you say.
>
> *"That decision was above my level,"* Ost says.

Cross looks at Maret.

> *"Did House Verath's survey team identify elevated permeability at the Ashford site eight months ago."*

Silence.

> *"Yes,"* Maret says.

> *"Did Verath notify the Lord Commander's office, or any coastal garrison, that a populated frontier settlement was built on a site with elevated undead permeability during a period of known binding degradation."*

Another silence.

> *"No,"* Maret says.

You say it quietly, to Cross, not to Maret.

> *"A village of forty-three people. Fourteen survivors."*

The room holds that number.

Cross's decision takes approximately four seconds.

> *"Your delegation's proposal is declined. House Verath's survey records — all of them, every site, every notation — will be transferred to the Lord Commander's office within thirty days. Your private military force will stand down pending a formal inquiry."*

Maret's composure holds to the end. It is an impressive thing to watch — the complete absence of visible defeat in a woman who has just watched forty years of her patron's work collapse in a command room. She nods once, correctly, and leads her delegation out.

Ost is last through the door. He pauses.

> *"The village,"* he says. *"Was it—"*
>
> *"Fourteen survivors,"* you say. *"Out of forty-three."*

He nods. Goes.

---

## Part Seven: The Survey

> *"Twenty-nine hollow points unmonitored across the central plains,"* Cross says. *"And an army about to march into them."*

She looks at you with the expression of a woman who has identified a resource and is about to make use of it.

You offer to help map them. You know the terrain. You've walked it. You know what the ground looks like when something is wrong with it before you know why.

> *"Give me that surveyor,"* you say.

Cross almost smiles.

She gives you Davin Ost. She gives you a commission from her office — garrison access at every post between here and the northern frontier. Forty marks a week, resupply from garrison stores at each waypoint. The army moves in three weeks. She needs the major sites along the primary campaign route identified and flagged before it does.

You start at the northern end and work south. The army marches north — you want the ground ahead of them cleared first.

---

You find Lenne and Edwick in the waiting room. Edwick is asleep with the boneless relief of a man who has finished carrying something very heavy. Lenne is awake, watching the door. She reads your face when you enter.

> *"It worked,"* she says.
>
> *"Mostly."*

You give her Cross's vessel authorization. She needs to go home. Her father needs to hear what happened from her, not from a rider's summary.

> *"You don't remember how this started,"* she says.
>
> *"No."*
>
> *"Ashford. The treeline. Fourteen arrows."*
>
> *"I have your account of it. In my coat."*

She looks at you for a long moment.

> *"I'll write the rest,"* she says. *"When I get back to Thornwall. Everything from the canal to this room. I'll send it north when it's done."*
>
> *"I may be hard to find."*
>
> *"You'll be on the campaign route between here and the northern frontier mapping hollow points. I'll find you."*

At the waiting room door, she turns back.

> *"The surveyor. Ost. Watch him."*
>
> *"I know."*
>
> *"Not because he testified against them. Because he didn't, for three years, and then he did, and you don't know yet which one is the exception and which one is the rule."*

She goes.

---

## Epilogue: The Campaign Route

You stand in the waiting room of the Greyvane garrison with a rolled survey map and a warm commission and a surveyor you don't fully trust and three weeks before an army marches north into unmapped ground.

Through the window the harbor is full of masts. The army of the coastal kingdoms, assembled at last, the largest force humanity has put in the field since the Empire of the Sun. Waiting to reclaim the heartland.

Waiting, it turns out, on information nobody has finished gathering yet.

You unfurl the survey map on the waiting room table and find the campaign route and start at the north end, where the frontier villages are sparse and the ground is open and whatever the Warden's seventeen years of degradation did to the soft places in the earth is still sitting there unmarked.

You begin to read the map the way you read a forest.

Looking for what belongs and what doesn't.

*The road is long.*

*There is work ahead.*

---

*End of Chapter Two*
