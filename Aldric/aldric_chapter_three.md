# Aldric — Chapter Three
## A Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Prologue

*The army muster fills Greyvane's harbor behind you as you ride north. Three weeks. The road ahead is pale and flat and the survey map is rolled in your saddlebag and Davin Ost rides two lengths back on a garrison horse, saying nothing.*

---

## Part One: The Survey Route

You let the silence run for an hour before you speak.

> *"The Ashford site,"* you say. *"Tell me what you measured."*

Ost explains resonance depth — how every hollow point has one, how far down the boundary sits, how easily it responds to surface conditions. Thornwall's well sits at four meters, stable, held in place by the binding for four thousand years. The Ashford site sits at point seven. Closer to the surface than any site in the survey record.

> *"What does that mean in practice?"*
>
> *"It means the boundary is thin enough that a significant enough event at the surface could open it without any deliberate working. Not a controlled opening. Just — a tear."*

The village was built eight months after Ost measured it. Then it was overrun. Forty-three people.

The first survey site north of Greyvane reads elevated — point three to one point one, nearly four times the previous measurement. The second site is fifty percent higher. The third is four and a half times its previous reading.

You spread the map on the farmstead's largest foundation stone and look at the three sites. Their coordinates. Their previous readings. Their current readings. Four minutes.

> *"They're not all rising at the same rate. The ones closest to Thornwall are rising faster."*

Ost goes very still. *"It's radiating. From the Thornwall site outward. Like a stone dropped in water."*

The binding renewal drew energy from the hollow point network. The renewal sealed Thornwall. But the energy that leaked out during the seventeen years of degradation is still propagating through every connected site across the entire central plains.

You write to Edwick from the farmstead ruins — the pattern, the readings, the questions only he can answer — and send the letter by garrison rider.

---

## Part Two: The Wandering Dead

Alone at the farmstead site while Ost rides to the watch post, you feel the wind shift. The particular quality of frontier nights when things are closer to the surface than they should be.

Something is moving in the grass two hundred meters north.

You track it by the grass movement. Single-sourced. Old dead, the wandering kind — out early, the site's elevation affecting the boundary enough to let them surface in marginal light. You wait for the clear angle. You loose.

The arrow goes true and the dead thing moves at the wrong moment. The shot takes it through the shoulder instead of the skull. It closes the distance before you can nock a third arrow.

The collision sends you sideways into the foundation stones. Your right knee takes the edge of a buried stone at the wrong angle. You drive the knife up through the jaw from the side.

It drops.

The knee is a Moderate injury worsening toward Severe. The palm is a graze from your own broadhead. You re-wrap and ride on because Edwick needs redirecting and the work doesn't wait.

---

## Part Three: The Village

The fourth site — northwest, three hours from camp, previously the highest reading in the survey outside of Thornwall — reads one point eight on arrival. Above the Ashford threshold. The grass around it the wrong color. No breach yet.

Half a kilometer north, barely visible through the morning fog: a frontier village. Too still.

You split the work — Ost takes detailed readings, you scout the village perimeter.

The fall happens east of the palisade. A ground-squirrel burrow, a misstep at a canter, and the knee that was Moderate becomes Severe in the space of the corrective movement. You come up with the bow already raised.

Three undead from the village gate. Open ground. Clear sightlines.

Three clean shots. Three bodies in the mud.

The knee accepts the brace Ost applies. It does not accept the decision to walk the perimeter afterward, but you walk it anyway because you need to know everything before you commit to a course of action.

On the south face of the palisade: a symbol scratched deep into the timber. Four vertical lines with a diagonal cross through them.

> *"That's a surveyor's mark,"* Ost says. *"Old notation. Pre-Verath. Means — site active, boundary breached, do not enter without preparation."*

Someone else was here. Someone who knew what they were looking at.

A single partial boot print at the edge of the churned area. Pointing south. Someone walked away from this village heading toward Greyvane.

---

## Part Four: The Physician

You ride for the garrison. Six kilometers. The knee graduates from Severe to Critical on the uneven ground east of the palisade — not a fall, just the wrong angle at the wrong moment, and the joint stops giving reliable feedback.

You stay mounted. Ride on. The watch post physician is named **Fera**.

She unwraps the brace, examines the joint in the afternoon light for a long time without speaking.

> *"You rode on this,"* she says.
>
> *"Yes."*
>
> *"After I told you not to."*
>
> *"Yes."*

Complete immobilization. Five days. Not three. Five.

> *"Whatever it is, it will still be there in five days. The knee may not."*

You send a rider to Cross with the site readings, the breached village, the sealed palisade, the surveyor's mark, the boot print heading south. Sergeant **Kort** confirms the garrison's patrol boundary doesn't cover that ground. Millford's garrison, short-staffed since the muster, last patrolled the western plain three or four weeks ago.

Cross's response arrives the next day with a garrison squad for the village and a postscript:

*Millford garrison reports a lone traveler passed through their post eight days ago — refused to give name, described as sixty, thin, ink-stained hands. Heading north.*

Edwick. On a boat to Thornwall with Lenne. Except not.

He left the vessel before Thornwall. He went north on his own reasoning before your letter arrived. Before you confirmed the pattern he already suspected.

Five days of enforced rest. The knee improves from Critical to Severe progressing. Fera agrees to come north as field physician.

Lenne arrives on day two, carrying a letter from Edwick's cabin on the vessel.

---

## Part Five: Edwick's Letter

*If the binding renewal draws from the network rather than from the speaker's memory alone — and I believe it does, the original texts suggest the memory sacrifice is a catalyst, not the fuel — then the propagation is not a side effect. It is the mechanism. The First Empire built the network to do exactly this. The binding doesn't just contain the Warden. It feeds it. And when the binding is renewed, the network feeds the renewal, and the renewal feeds the network, and the cycle completes.*

*The cycle was designed to complete once. Catastrophically. When the network has given everything it has.*

*I don't know what happens when the network empties. I'm afraid I do know.*

*I should have understood this in the tower. I should have stopped it. I didn't.*

*I'm going north.*

Edwick thinks he caused this. By designing the renewal. By teaching you the words.

On day five, Fera replaces the splint with a fitted brace and makes her assessment: Severe, improved from Critical. Function restored. The next failure is permanent.

You ride north with Lenne, Fera, Vel, and Tomas.

---

## Part Six: Ashford

Ashford appears in the grey morning light — the palisade, the closed gate, the garrison pennant. East of it, the dead grass circle, larger than the survey data suggested. At its center, the ordinary-looking patch of ground that isn't ordinary.

And outside the palisade: **Davin Ost**, **Edwick**, **Lenne**.

Edwick looks worse than three days should produce. He's been awake most of the time. Six meters from the dead grass edge. Too close.

The measurements: door is 5.1 meters. Growth rate has increased. The approaching mass is detectable at 40 meters depth. Closing rate approximately 3.5 meters per hour.

You do the arithmetic. Forty meters. Three and a half meters per hour.

Eleven hours.

Edwick's account:

> *"The inversion. It's not the hollow point changing direction. It's a second source. Something came up through the hollow point inside the wall. It moved. And where it stopped — it made a new one."*
>
> *"What was below the network."*
>
> *"The network is old. Older than the Empire of the Sun. Older than the First Empire. The hollow points aren't natural formations — they were made. By something that needed them. A system, a structure, something that connected every soft place in the plains into a single working."*

**Ost:**

> *"The network isn't an antenna. It's a cage. And the door isn't something being made from outside."*

**Edwick:**

> *"It's being opened from inside."*

You send Tomas south with everything. Then you establish the perimeter and wait.

---

## Part Seven: The Door Opens

Five and a half hours. The closing rate accelerates.

You position everyone — Ost north with instruments, Fera north mobile, Vel west between door and palisade, Tomas east, Lenne north fifteen meters back. You and Edwick south, twenty meters from the dead grass edge. The hunter's position. Downwind.

From Edwick, in the final hours:

The cage-builders' final notation. His translation:

*What sleeps below the seals is not evil. It is appetite. It does not hate. It does not love. It is the oldest hunger and the oldest dark and it was here before the light came and it will be here after. We built the cage because we had to. We maintain the cage because we must. We ask those who come after to understand: it cannot be destroyed. It can only be contained. The cage must hold. The cage must always hold.*

The dead things inside the palisade go silent. Then begin moving — toward the hollow point beneath the village. Not toward the gate. Down.

The door opens.

Not dramatically. A long, slow exhalation of cold air moving across the plains in every direction. The smell of deep stone and absolute dark and time measured in geological rather than human terms. The circle of dead grass sinking half a meter.

The first one comes through.

Child-sized, roughly. Two limbs, two limbs, upright posture — but the proportions wrong in ways that are hard to locate precisely. Skin the color of deep cave stone. Large dark eyes, systematic, reading the world.

It looks at you.

A second emerges. A third. A fourth.

All of them face northwest. Toward the Sylvan Deep. Toward the stasis working that has been suppressing them from above for ten thousand years.

You withdraw the group to one hundred meters and hold.

Tomas rides south with everything you know. The door stands open. The beings stand and face northwest with the patience of things that are very good at waiting.

---

## Part Eight: The Sylvan Deep

Two days north. The boreal belt. The transition from plains to forest to something older and more complete — the specific quality of air that has been filtered through ten thousand years of living wood.

You feel the stasis working before you see evidence of it. Not as a sensation. As an absence. The world held in the specific moment of its own perfection by something that has been refusing to let it change since before human civilization existed.

At the grove's boundary, a figure steps out from between two perfect trees. Tall. Silver-haired. Moving with the quality described as *too precise.*

An elf. The first you've seen.

> *"Why should we speak with you."*
>
> *"Someone had to come,"* you say. *"I was the one who could."*

You tell them about the door. About the nine emerged. About the stasis working's thinning note. The elf listens.

> *"We felt the door open. Three days ago."*
>
> *"And you didn't send anyone south."*
>
> *"We do not send anyone anywhere. We have not sent anyone anywhere in four hundred years."*

The working is weakening. The elf confirms it has been weakening for three centuries. The renewal accelerated the timeline considerably.

> *"How long."*
>
> *"Weeks,"* it says. *"Perhaps less."*

You tell them Edwick's theory — the stasis working and the hollow point network as two layers of the same containment. The elf goes very still.

The third elf — the oldest voice, the one that sits in the chest rather than the ear — brings you inside the grove.

The stasis working hits you like stepping into still water. Your knee, in this air, stops complaining. Not healed — but quiet.

At the base of the oldest tree, pressing both palms flat against the largest root, you receive the transmission:

The cage-builders tried to speak with the beings before building the cage. Many times. They tried offering what the beings needed. They tried bargaining. They tried retreat. It wasn't possible — not because the beings refused, not because they were hostile, but because they couldn't negotiate. They were of the surface. All of it. Partial return wasn't return at all.

The cage was not the first response. It was the last one. After everything else failed.

And in the oldest layer of the transmission, placed in the root by the beings themselves:

*We will wait. The surface changes. Perhaps what comes after will find a way that we could not.*

---

## Part Nine: Root

The third elf agrees to come south.

> *"Call me Root,"* it says. *"I have been keeping watch over something sleeping for a very long time."*

One day in the grove. The knee improves marginally in the stasis working's stillness. Then you ride south together.

On the road, Root talks.

> *"They are not from below. The common assumption — that they came from underground — is incorrect. The network didn't imprison something that lived below the plains. It drew something down from the surface and held it there."*
>
> *"They were here before us. Before the First Empire. Before the orcs and goblins. Before the dwarfs arrived in their mountains. They were the surface of this world in its first age — not living on it, not beneath it. They were of it, the way weather is of a world."*

Something arrived. Something from outside this world. It found the surface already occupied and built the network to draw the original inhabitants downward. Then maintained the outer suppression.

And that something built the socket knowing something like the elves would come to fill it.

> *"We were so afraid of repeating what we had done that we overcorrected into doing nothing,"* Root says. *"The beings below asked for what comes after to find a way. We were what came after. We didn't look for a way. We just held the door."*
>
> *"You're looking now,"* you say.
>
> *"Yes,"* it says. *"I am looking now."*

---

## Part Ten: The Question

Ashford. Afternoon light. Seventeen emerged beings facing south — toward you.

One steps forward. Fifty meters. It transmits.

*You are the one who came looking.*

*We left it for you. Not for you specifically. For whoever would come. We did not know if anyone would come.*

*The one beside you has been holding the door closed for ten thousand years. We know its hands. We know the weight of them. We do not resent the weight.*

Root receives a transmission of its own. Something private and old that passes between them in the language of ten thousand years of contact.

Then the being asks — not with words, with the whole quality of the transmission, the entire weight of what they are, pressed into a single uncertainty:

*Is there room for what we are in what the world has become.*

You transmit back honestly.

*I don't know. I don't know enough to answer that honestly. What I know is that the question is worth asking. And that the people who need to be part of answering it aren't all here yet.*

The beings will hold. They will stay at the door. They will not advance. Root will maintain the working alone — months, perhaps, drawn carefully.

*Do not let the army come north. We do not want to hurt what lives on the surface. But we will not go back below. The door does not close from our side.*

*Find the way. We will wait for it.*

---

## Epilogue: The Question Goes South

At the watch post, Edwick arrives riding north, redirected by your letter. He is road-dirty and carrying three new pages of notes written in the saddle. He sees Root and stops his horse.

Root looks at him.

> *"You are the one who designed the renewal."*
>
> *"Yes."*
>
> *"The beings below asked me to thank you. For opening the door. They have been waiting for it to open for ten thousand years."*

Edwick sits down on the ground beside the road and puts his face in his hands and stays there for thirty seconds.

When he looks up his eyes are dry.

Around the fire that night, Root and Edwick talk — the scholar who understands the architecture of the cage and the elf who built the outer layer of it, comparing notes across a ten-thousand-year gap.

You listen. You think about six days. About the Lord Commander two days south. About orcs on the central plains and their Rememberers who carry the most detailed account of what the world looked like before anyone else arrived. About dwarfs in mountains that sit directly above the hollow point network.

The answer to *is there room* is not a six-day answer. But six days is enough to reach the Lord Commander and hold the army. And holding the army buys the time the answer actually needs.

One thing at a time.

Somewhere two hundred kilometers north, seventeen beings stand in the dead grass at the edge of an open door and wait with the patience of things that have already demonstrated they can wait for a very long time — and have decided, against all expectation, to hope.

*Perhaps what comes after will find a way.*

You came looking. That's the first step.

---

*End of Chapter Three*
