# Aldric — Chapter One
## A Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Prologue

*You started in a village called Ashford, at the treeline before dawn, with the gate hanging open and the smell of rot on the wind. You had fourteen arrows and a hunting knife and eight silver pennies and you went in anyway.*

---

## Part One: Ashford

The smell hits you before anything else.

Rot and woodsmoke, mingling in the grey pre-dawn air. You're crouched at the treeline a hundred meters from Ashford, the village of forty-odd souls you've called home for three seasons. The palisade gate hangs open — wrong, badly wrong — and the mud between here and there is churned into black furrows.

You were out overnight tracking a boar. Whatever happened here, happened while you slept under pine needles.

A single torch still burns on the gatehouse post. By its light you can make out a shape slumped against the inner wall. Could be a body. Could be a survivor. Beyond the gate, something moves — slow, dragging — in the courtyard shadows.

From the treeline your hunter's patience pays back every second you give it. You sink lower into the ferns, slow your breathing, and let your eyes do the work. The courtyard resolves itself piece by piece. Three of them — shambling, trailing grave-cloth, the too-loose gait of the dead. Not freshly turned. These are old things, the wandering kind that crawl out of the plains soil some nights when the wind is wrong.

One of the three undead is tangled in the bucket rope at the well, circling uselessly. Two remain mobile. The torch on the gatehouse post sits directly above a loose bundle of thatch. The slumped figure by the wall is alive — **Maret**, the miller's daughter. Sixteen years old.

The villagers didn't all die. A dozen sets of boot prints lead north, toward the old mill road. Someone organized an escape. They left Maret behind.

You sprint through the gate. At ten meters there is no missing — the first arrow drives through the nearest dead thing's skull above the ear and it drops like its strings were cut. The second arrow punches through a shoulder instead of a skull, spinning it sideways. It doesn't go down. A hand closes around your bow arm — cold and absolute, fingers like iron bar stock. Teeth scrape your collar by a knuckle's width.

Maret hits it from behind with a fence post she's pulled from somewhere. Once. Twice. The third blow drops it.

She's breathing in ragged pulls, standing over the corpse, the post slick in her hands. The well-rope snaps somewhere behind you. The third one is free.

You grab her wrist and run.

The darkness of the pines swallows you both and you keep going until the village is just a smear of orange light through the trunks.

> *"North,"* she says, before you can ask. *"My father took them north. Old Henric's mill, maybe two leagues. There were six of them. The dead. We only saw three."*

Three unaccounted for.

---

## Part Two: The Mill Road

You tell Maret to stay put and quiet, back against a thick pine, fence post across her lap.

The mill road is a pale scar through the forest, moonlight catching the wheel ruts. You find the first sign easily enough — boot tracks in the soft verge, a dozen people moving fast. Then you find the other tracks. Two sets. Bare feet, dragging at the heel. Following your people.

You're reading the second set of prints when you feel it before you see it. A weight in the air to your left. That sweet-rot smell.

It's standing four meters away between two pines, perfectly still, head tilted at the wrong angle, watching you with eyes that catch no light at all.

You don't give it the moment it needs. Three steps and you're on it, knife low, driving up under the jaw with everything your legs can give. The blade punches through the base of the skull and the thing's arms go slack in the same instant. You lower it to the pine needles without a sound.

Four down. Two on the road ahead. The sixth remains a ghost.

You set an ambush at a bend in the road where a deadfall narrows the path to single-file. Maret flat behind the fallen trunk. You in the crook of a pine. The dead come around the bend exactly as expected — Jorin, who traded you bad grain last autumn, and a woman in a nightgown. The first arrow takes Jorin in the chest instead of the skull. He staggers. Maret erupts from behind the deadfall with the post. You drop Jorin from above. The woman falls to your knife.

An owl calls somewhere behind you. Except there are no owls on the plains.

The sixth dead thing is standing at the village treeline, staring at the orange smear of the dying torch. You track it by bent stem and disturbed moss. You find it. You draw to your cheek, breathe out half a breath, hold the hollow of the hold, and wait for the head to turn just enough.

It turns. You loose.

Six dead things came out of the plains tonight. Six dead things are back in the ground.

---

## Part Three: Henric's Mill

Fourteen survivors from a village of forty-three.

Osbert, the miller's brother, looks at you with the particular expression of a man who needs something done and has identified the person least likely to refuse.

> *"Road south is finished. Three more villages between here and Thornwall. Someone needs to ride ahead. Warn them, or find out if there's anything left to warn. There's silver in it. Forty marks if you reach Thornwall and bring back word."*

Forty marks is four months of honest work.

You leave at first light.

---

## Part Four: The Road South

The first village, **Crale's Ford**, is empty. Not attacked — abandoned. Doors left open, a pot still hanging over a cold hearth, chickens wandering the main street.

**Dunmore** tells a different story. Five, maybe six undead moving between the buildings. A survivor on the inn roof — a man named **Bram** — waving one arm in slow, exhausted arcs.

You pick off three from the treeline. The fourth arrow cracks against a doorframe and everything locks onto the sound. You fall back to the trees, funnel them single-file through a gap between two fallen logs. Two drop clean. One gets through. It closes the gap and you drive your knife up through its jaw.

Bram comes down from the roof. Fifty, broad-shouldered going to fat, with the permanent squint of a man who reads weather. He holds a woodsman's hatchet and doesn't put it down when he sees you.

> *"How many you?"*
> *"One."*
> *"You clear them all?"*
> *"Six. Yes."*

He tells you something a drunk tinker said four days ago: the Thornwall gates were still open. But something large was moving on the road in daylight.

You find it an hour from Dunmore. A plains troll — eight feet of grey-green muscle and bad intent, knuckles dragging, heading north. You go flat in the grass and count footsteps by the vibrations in the ground and wait while its shadow passes over you and it pauses and smells the air and moves on.

When it's gone Bram sits up the color of old wax and says: *"Right."*

On the road into Dunmore you find a merchant's guild seal pressed in brass — a tower stamped into its face — lying in a dark smear on the road. Someone was coming north from Thornwall. The troll was fed and unhurried.

**Harrow Cross** has been abandoned — not attacked, just emptied. At the granary, a barred door. Children's voices. You don't try to open it.

> *"There's a mill two leagues north,"* you tell the door. *"Henric's Mill. Fourteen people from Ashford. Follow the wheel ruts. The dead things on the road are cleared. That part's done."*

You don't wait for more. You walk south.

Thornwall's walls appear on the horizon. Forty feet of stone, torches lit along the battlements. The torches are burning. Someone is still up there to light them.

The gate guards are not interested in you. You hold up the brass seal and make them interested.

They make you wait twenty minutes. Full dark arrives. Then boots on stone steps and the small door set into the gate opens. A woman. Forty, soldier-lean. Officer's insignia on her collar. She looks at you. At Bram. At your wrist and your cheekbone and the bloodstains on your leather.

> *"Come inside. There's a physic for the wrist. And you'll want a drink before I tell you what's happening in this city."*

That last part lands wrong. Not an offer — a warning.

The streets of Thornwall are quieter than a city this size should be at nightfall. Soldiers on every corner. And underneath the tallow smoke, faintly:

*Sweetrot.*

It's inside the walls.

---

## Part Five: Thornwall

Her name is **Captain Sera Undh**. Three nights ago something got into the merchant quarter. Eleven missing. Three confirmed dead, turned and burned before anyone understood. One of the barricades moved from the inside.

The Lord Commander's daughter lives in the merchant quarter. House of the goldsmith, northeast corner. Her name is **Lenne**.

One hundred and twenty silver marks if you bring her out. You negotiate it down to eighty and a horse.

You enter through the canal — knee-deep water, cold enough to ache, under the wall in the dark. You read the courtyard in twenty seconds: the dead are orbiting. A slow, loose circuit around the trading hall at the quarter's heart. The streets between here and the goldsmith's house sit on the outside of that orbit. Relatively clear.

Bram's mental map is twelve years old and the alley he remembers has been bricked up. You find this out in a dead end with footsteps behind you, two sets, ten meters and closing. You press Bram against the wall and find his wrist in the dark when his grip tightens on the hatchet and squeeze once. He understands. The dead thing at the alley mouth breathes in. Breathes out. Moves on.

You find the goldsmith's house. Iron shutters. Tall and narrow. A thread of candlelight so thin you'd miss it if you weren't looking.

*Clever.*

The pebble skips off the shutter frame and the candlelight vanishes. Something closes the half-block gap fast. You raise the bow, pull to your cheek, and when the dead thing rounds the corner you put an arrow through its temple at ten meters. It folds without a sound. The splint cracks.

Above you the shutter opens one inch. An eye in the gap. Young. Watchful.

> *"Undh sent you,"* she says. Not a question.
> *"Your father sent me. One-twenty marks if I bring you out."*
> *A pause. "He only has eighty."*
> *"Then I'll settle for eighty and a horse. Come down. We're leaving."*

Lenne is not what you expected. Dressed for movement. Dark wool, hair tied back. In her right hand is a cobbler's awl she's been sharpening on the windowsill for three days.

> *"You're badly hurt,"* she says.
> *"Yes,"* you say. *"Move."*

She moves.

You find a merchant in shock in the service lane — alive, pressed against the wall. Lenne says his name and he swallows the scream. You jerk your head north. He comes.

Four of you down into the black water. Through the grate. Up into cold night air on the far side of the wall.

All four. Alive.

> *"Eighty marks,"* Lenne says.
> *"And the horse,"* you agree.

---

## Part Six: The Warden

The merchant quarter is not done with Thornwall.

Something came up through a well beneath the trading hall. Not the shambling kind. Something that stands at the center of the dead's orbit like a lodestone — that uses a key to enter an archive at three in the morning, that reads city surveys in the dark and replaces the volumes not quite flush with the shelf.

A figure. Old beyond reckoning. Dressed in the manner of a civilization that ended before your great-great-grandmother was born. Pale light in its chest like embers through a grate.

**The First Warden.** Bound four thousand years ago by the priests of the first human empire. Not a sorcerer-king — older than the empire, something the empire *found* when they first broke ground. They bound it because they couldn't move it and couldn't destroy it.

The binding requires renewal every thousand years. The last renewal was nine hundred and eighty years ago, by accident, during the sun-rite that ended the old empire — all that living belief pouring into the ground, feeding the binding another thousand years without anyone knowing. Now it's seventeen years overdue and degrading.

**House Verath** — old money, forty years of recovering things from desert ruins — found the original binding documents. They want the Warden bound to their will. An army of the dead, directed. *The plains are full of bones.*

They hired a scholar named **Pol Edwick** to speak the renewal. Consent of the living — that's all the binding needs. Whoever speaks it, the Warden binds to them.

You track the Warden through Thornwall to an old harbor tower standing in water — the Verath seal on its door. You come up the stairs heel-toe, bow up, Lenne behind you, and find Edwick reading aloud from an open book, three words from the end of the sentence.

You shoot the book.

The arrow takes the volume clean out of his hands and pins it to the window shutter. The words — whatever words remained — go unspoken.

The Warden turns. Its eyes find you across the room. The pale light in its chest dims slightly, then holds. It looks at you with the patience of something that has been waiting four thousand years and can wait a little longer.

Then it speaks. Old tongue, layered, arriving in your chest before your ears process it. Underneath the oldness, like the common tongue heard through water, three words:

> *Not yet. Again.*

It steps backward through the window. Not falling. *Stepping.* The pale light descends to the harbor water and is gone.

---

Edwick sits on the stone floor with his ink-stained hands in his lap. He tells you everything.

There is another way to renew the binding. Without consent. The original method requires three things: a place of living power, the words of the old tongue, and a sacrifice of memory. Whoever has witnessed the Warden directly speaks the words and gives the memory of those encounters to the binding as fuel.

The cost: those specific memories don't return. Whatever makes you most qualified to perform the rite is precisely what the rite removes.

The place of living power is the hollow point beneath the trading hall. The speaker must go down to the well.

You are the most qualified person in Thornwall. You have witnessed the Warden directly. You carry the encounters in living tissue.

You don't let the silence get comfortable enough to fill itself with someone else's voice.

> *"I'll do it,"* you say.

---

## Part Seven: The Well

Three days.

Edwick transcribes the words by candlelight. You spend the first day resting — really resting — your wrist finishing its healing the proper way. You buy arrows. Twenty, broadhead, the good kind. You spend an hour on the garrison range re-educating the joint to the draw. By the end your groupings are where they should be.

Lenne finds you at the range on the afternoon of the second day.

> *"You know you won't remember any of this."*
> *"I know."*
> *"The villages. The troll. The canal. The archive. The tower."*
> *"I know."*
> *"That's a significant portion of the last five days."*
> *"It is."*

She watches you shoot for a while.

> *"I'll remember it,"* she says. *"All of it. I'll write it down if you want."*

You lower the bow. *"Write it down,"* you say.

---

The third day. You leave the seventy-six marks on the garrison cot. Lenne hands you a folded paper — the first page of what she's written.

Bram is at the garrison door. You shake his hand.

> *"There's a village at Henric's Mill,"* you say. *"Fourteen people. They'll need word about the road."*
> *"I'll go,"* he says.

You walk into the merchant quarter.

The Warden's dead part before you.

You walk through the merchant quarter and the dead things in their slow rotation turn their faces away and let you pass and you arrive at the trading hall door without incident.

Inside, the hall is dark and cold and vast. At the center, the well. Stone. Old. The mortar a different color from everything built after. A darkness below that isn't about distance but about time.

You sit on the well's edge. Read the transcription once more. The words are where you put them.

Then you say them.

The old tongue sits in your mouth like river water — cold, moving, older than the channel it runs through. The darkness below responds. The Warden rises through the well as it descended through the harbor window — with the ease of something that knows where all the floors are. It stands before you, pale light in its chest, four-thousand-year eyes reading your face.

You say the last words.

The binding takes.

You feel it leave — not painfully, not like loss, more like setting down a weight you'd carried long enough that you'd stopped noticing its shape. Something goes out of you into the stone and the dark and the old language and the Warden's eyes, and the pale light in its chest steadies and brightens and then dims to something sustainable. A coal instead of a fire. A presence instead of an urgency.

The dead things in the merchant quarter stop moving.

The Warden looks at you for a long moment.

Then it descends. Back into the dark. Back into the binding that will hold now for another thousand years.

*The well is just a well.*

You sit on the stone edge and try to remember what you came here to do.

---

## Epilogue: The South Road

You don't know who Undh is. She gives you a document — safe passage through the coastal checkpoints, a letter of introduction to the garrison commander at Saltmere. *In case you need work.*

You find the horse in the stable. The roan regards you with the calm of an animal that has been through enough to develop perspective.

Lenne is at the south gate with a pack and her awl and a dark bay horse of her own.

You ride through the south gate into the afternoon. The road opens before you, pale and straight, the sea smell strengthening with every kilometer.

You ride for an hour in comfortable silence.

> *"You don't remember any of it,"* she says.
> *"No."*
> *"Does that bother you?"*

You consider the question honestly.

> *"I remember how to do things,"* you say. *"That's what I need."*

She looks at you sideways. *"You also saved a city. Possibly more than one."*

> *"You remember that."*
> *"Yes."*
> *"Then it happened,"* you say. *"That's enough."*

You reach inside your coat and find the folded paper.

*You started in a village called Ashford,* it reads, *at the treeline before dawn, with the gate hanging open and the smell of rot on the wind. You had fourteen arrows and a hunting knife and eight silver pennies and you went in anyway.*

You fold it back up and put it away.

The road runs south.

Behind you, beneath the streets of Thornwall, in the dark below a trading hall well, something old and patient sits in its renewed binding and waits. The world will change above it — cities rising and falling, empires waking and sleeping, the plains filling with people and emptying again.

On the south road, two riders move toward the sea. One of them carries a story the other one can't remember. The other one carries everything he needs.

*The road is long.*

*There is work ahead.*

---

*End of Chapter One*
