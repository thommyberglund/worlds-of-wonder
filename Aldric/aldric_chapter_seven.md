# Aldric — Chapter Seven
## A Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Prologue

*Winter comes to the central plains.*

*Not dramatically — the frontier doesn't do dramatic seasons, it does gradual ones. The yellow grass going grey-brown. The mornings arriving with frost on the palisade timber. The sky dropping to a flat white that sits on the horizon like a lid.*

*You stay at the watch post and you work.*

---

## Part One: The Winter's Work

The first month is letters.

You write to everyone Edwick identified as capable of holding the long view inside the coastal kingdoms' institutions. Not the lords — the administrators and advisors and clerks who actually run the machinery of governance, the people who will need to understand the situation before their principals can be brought to a meeting.

You write badly at first. Too long, too technical. Edwick corrects them. Lenne corrects them with the instinct of someone who has watched her father receive information for seventeen years and knows what cuts through.

By the sixth week the letters are working. The responses that come back are not acknowledgments but questions — the specific questions of people who have read something and want to understand it more rather than less.

The Fate's priesthood is the most responsive. Not the most resistant as you expected — the most fascinated. The questions they send are sophisticated and specific and several come with invitations to visit.

You file the invitations for spring.

---

Kael and Route meet at Ashford on the fourteenth day of the first month.

The meeting lasts three days.

What they discover: four of the Deeproute clan's cave passages connect directly to Deepstone unconstructed levels that the Deepstone clan hasn't officially mapped. The connections exist. Neither side knew — the dwarfs stopped at the official boundary of their surveys and the goblins stopped at the edge of the dwarven-worked stone, each assuming the other side was closed to them.

Fera's report, arriving six days later:

*The routes and the halls are already connected. They just didn't know.*

You write to Edwick: *The framework doesn't need to create the underground connections. It needs to acknowledge the ones that already exist. The infrastructure is further along than anyone knew.*

Edwick writes back: *The same is apparently true in the eastern range. Brek reports that the Stonepath clan's initial survey shows six existing connections. Neither side knew. I am beginning to think the network built this deliberately.*

You write back: *Of course it did.*

---

Root's letter arrives in the second month. Three pages, handwriting so precise it looks like engraving.

*The Lord Commander is a remarkable man. He has asked me eleven hundred and forty-three questions in six days.*

*The eastern range clan-council — the Irongate clan — has agreed to attend the elder-council as an observer. The eastern range has its own records. They have been sharing them with no one for two hundred years.*

*— Root*

*P.S. The Lord Commander has a daughter. She asks about you. I have told her you are functional. She seemed to find this insufficient.*

Lenne's letter in the second month:

*My father has convened an informal council. The Fate's high priesthood, three coastal kingdom lords, the Greyvane merchant council's senior factor, and — his idea — a Rememberer. He invited Long Memory from Ashford.*

*Long Memory came.*

*The first time in eight hundred years that an orc Rememberer has attended a formal human governance meeting. The meeting lasted two days. They agreed to receive the framework when it is ready.*

*Long Memory told the oral account of the undead surge. The high priestess of the Fate wept.*

*Long Memory has a name in the Common Tongue. My father calls it that and it accepts the translation.*

*P.S. Root says hello. Root has been teaching my father elven. My father is a terrible student but he is trying.*

---

The third month brings **Lord Harwick Crale** — whose family name comes from Crale's Ford, the abandoned frontier village on the road to Thornwall.

His letter is not hostile. It is the letter of a man trying to understand something in good faith. His position: the framework proposes that humans eventually vacate the central plains. His family has held frontier land for three generations. They pushed into the frontier at significant cost. He wants to know what they reclaimed it for, if the answer is eventually to give it back.

A fair question.

You write him the direct answer.

*The plains will change. Over the course of the migration — measured in generations, not years — the central plains will return to something closer to what they were before the first cage was built.*

*The honest answer to what the frontier settlers reclaimed is this: a road. You pushed the frontier north and built the supply lines and established the waypoints and cleared the ground of the wandering dead, and all of that work was not for nothing — it was the infrastructure of the transition. The roads that carried colonists north will carry people west toward the mountain halls.*

*Your family's three generations on the frontier will be part of the record that determines how the transition is phased — the colonists who came furthest out should be, and will be, offered the earliest specific places inside.*

*I am not asking you to be glad about this. I am asking you to understand it clearly enough to help shape it.*

You wait eleven days for a response.

*You have the diplomatic instincts of a siege weapon. In my family that is a compliment.*

*You're right about the road. I don't like it. But I recognize that not liking something and it being wrong are different things.*

*Two things. First: Lady Morwen Ashe — eastern corridor lord, more skeptical than I am but more organized. If she can be brought to supportive skepticism, the governing bodies will move. I will speak with her.*

*Second: the colonists currently on the frontier need to know the timeline before the framework is presented formally. Someone who has been there. Someone who has walked those roads.*

*I think you know which situation produces a better outcome.*

*— H. Crale*

He's right. You write to Long Memory. You write to Maret.

---

## Part Two: Maret

You find her in the south meadow at Henric's Mill, directing three people in the laying of furrow lines, trying to get winter wheat established in cold ground.

She looks up. Looks at Long Memory behind you. Looks back at you.

> *"The hunter,"* she says. Not warmly, not coldly. *"You don't remember me."*
>
> *"No. I have an account of you. A girl with a fence post."*

Something moves in her face — not offense. The look of someone reduced to a phrase who finds the phrase acceptable because it's accurate.

She agrees to the circuit. Gives you until morning to arrange the planting.

You fall into step beside her on the furrow lines. She keeps working while you talk.

> *"Why did you stay on the frontier. After."*
>
> *"Because the people who left went back to the coast. And the coast is full of people who talk about reclaiming the plains in warm rooms with full plates. I wanted to be one of the people actually doing it. Not because I thought I was better than the ones who left. Because I couldn't make myself stop."*

You ask how the framework sits with her.

> *"It's not what I wanted. And it's what's true. And those are both real things and I am not going to pretend one of them isn't."*

She asks for one commitment: that the frontier settlers — the actual settlers, not lords and councils — get a real choice about where they go.

> *"That is exactly what the framework is supposed to guarantee. And Dura Stoneback — a dwarven elder who dissented specifically to protect voluntary compliance — is on the oversight body to make sure it stays guaranteed."*
>
> *"That's an honest answer,"* she says. *"Better than a comfortable one."*

At the end of the conversation:

> *"The fence post. The account says I hit it three times. It was four. The account got it wrong. I've never corrected anyone because it seemed small. But if you're telling the settlers what actually happened, it should be four."*
>
> *"Four,"* you say.
>
> *"Four,"* she confirms.

---

## Part Three: The Spring Circuit

Six villages. Eight weeks. Long Memory and two warriors. Maret. Fera. You.

At each village, the same structure: Maret opens. You follow. Long Memory speaks the long view.

Dunmore is first. Fourteen people at the well. The woman named **Talla** who planted the fence around the well herself, six years a settler, who asks better questions than most of the people who will vote on the framework.

Maret's opening at Dunmore:

> *"I'm from Ashford. Village down the road. It fell the autumn before last. I'm the one who hit the last dead thing with a fence post. Four times. The account says three but it was four."*

Someone makes the sound of a person recognizing a specific kind of frontier truth.

Long Memory's address:

> *"You are the newest people on the oldest land. The beings below the plains came here because it was their home and they are finally returning to it. You are not enemies. You are both people who needed the plains and arrived when you could. The difference is the cage held them below and let you above, and now the cage is open."*
>
> *"What does it require?"* Talla asks.
>
> *"Patience. And the willingness to grieve what you thought you were getting before you can accept what you are actually getting. And the knowledge that what you are actually getting, while it is not what you hoped for, is real and specific and built by people who are trying to make it right."*

Three hours. Practical questions. What happens to settlements during the migration period. What about above-ground options in the mountains. What about families who span generations — will children face the transition themselves.

You answer what you can. You say *the framework doesn't have that answer yet* where it doesn't.

At the end, Talla:

> *"The four hits. With the fence post. Why does the difference matter to you?"*
>
> *"Because it happened. And things that happened should be recorded correctly. Even small things. Especially small things. The whole framework we're talking about is built on the idea that what actually happened matters. Four hits matters because truth matters. That's all."*

---

Route finds you at the fourth village. A transmission from slightly south of where you'd expect the Deeproute clan.

*The circuit is working. Three of the other deep clans have asked us about it. They want to know what you are telling the surface people.*

*Tell them,* you transmit. *Whatever they want to know.*

*They want to know if the surface people are afraid.*

You think about Talla. About Maret. About fourteen people who listened for three hours and asked practical questions.

*Some of them. But they're trying anyway. That's what the surface people do.*

*Yes,* Route transmits. *We have noticed.*

---

Fera drafts three pages of healthcare provisions unprompted — what the medical needs of a transitioning population look like, what the framework must guarantee. You add them to the circuit testimony document.

Long Memory writes an account of what it has observed from eight centuries of watching surface people navigate hardship. *Humans have moved before. Every time they arrived somewhere new, the new place became home. The mountain halls will become home in the same way.*

The document that results is forty pages. You title it: *What the Settlers Said.*

---

## Part Four: Lord Crale and Pen

Crale's letter arrives at the mill after the circuit:

*I will meet you at the mountain halls' eastern approach in six days. Lady Ashe will not be coming to the drafting session. She will be coming to the presentation — when the framework is complete and ready. She says she does not contribute to documents she has not decided to support, and she has not decided yet.*

*This is the best possible version of Lady Ashe's response. She has not said no. She has said she needs to see the finished product. That is a door.*

*I am bringing my steward Pen, who will ask questions about implementation that none of you will have thought of. Treat his objections as gifts.*

Pen reads *What the Settlers Said* on horseback over two days. His finding: the settlers' concern about allocation voice is structural. The oversight body will not give them enough votes. The solution is a separate frontier settlers' consultation process with a formal veto right on community-specific allocation decisions.

> *"Talla at Dunmore mentioned this, actually. Not in those words. But the concern is exactly right."*
>
> *"Talla should be on that body,"* you say.
>
> *"Talla,"* Pen says, and writes it in the margin. *"She should be on the settlers' consultation body."*

---

## Part Five: The Framework Drafted

The mountain halls. The records room. Edwick, Kael, Sera, Brek, Lord Crale, Pen, Maret, Long Memory.

Edwick puts the framework on the table. One hundred and twelve pages. Six months.

You put *What the Settlers Said* beside it.

The drafting takes four days.

Pen finds seven sentences that sound complete but leave implementation gaps. He rewrites them.

Crale contributes the human texture — every time the drafting produces language that is technically correct but emotionally evasive, he reads it and says *what this actually means for my family is* — and Edwick rewrites the language to say what Crale said.

Maret contributes two things.

First: a paragraph about people in transition — who have left one place and not yet arrived at another, the particular population of the frontier who are never quite settled and will need special consideration. Edwick had not thought of them. Pen had not thought of them. Maret had been one of them for two years.

Second: the title.

*The Long Negotiation: A Framework for the Voluntary Migration of Surface-Dwelling Peoples to the Mountain Halls of the Western and Eastern Ranges, in Acknowledgment of the Prior Claim of the Original Inhabitants of the Central Plains.*

> *"That's very long,"* Edwick says.
>
> *"It's a long negotiation,"* she says.

---

On the fourth day, the unresolved question.

The First Emperors. The sorcerer-kings in the desert tombs. The second phase. The framework addresses the migration and the beings. It does not address what happens when the desert blooms again.

> *"The framework cannot solve that,"* Pen says. *"The framework is for the migration."*
>
> *"But the framework should acknowledge that it exists,"* Edwick says.

You:

> *"Add an appendix. One page. Acknowledging the existence of the second phase without pretending to solve it. We don't have to solve everything. We have to solve what's in front of us and be honest about what we're leaving for the people who come after."*

Edwick: *"The cage-builders' notation. 'We ask those who come after to understand.'"*

> *"Yes. And now we are those who come after. And we are asking the same of the people who come after us."*

Pen writes the appendix in twenty minutes. One page. Clear. Honest:

*This framework does not address the second phase of the long negotiation, which concerns the eventual waking of the First Emperors of the Deep South and the continued decline of the elven stasis working. These matters are acknowledged as real, significant, and beyond the scope of this document. They are the responsibility of future councils.*

Everyone signs it.

That evening, Edwick pours something he has been keeping for an occasion. Maret looks at the signed framework.

> *"The winter wheat took. Finally. Third week of the circuit."* A pause. *"We plant things. We see if they take. And if they don't take this generation, the next one tries again."*
>
> *"That is more or less the entire framework,"* Edwick says.

---

## Part Six: Thornwall

Three months to the formal presentation. You spend the first in Thornwall.

A priest named **Aldric Vann** has been developing a theological position for fifteen years: that the Fate's power derives not from individual human belief but from the accumulated belief of every living thing in a place. Considered heterodox. Not widely circulated.

When you tell him about the hollow point network, he goes very still.

> *"The old prayers. 'May this ground remember you.' We say it as metaphor."*
>
> *"It's not a metaphor,"* you say.

Vann writes three papers over the winter. The third — *The Inhabited Ground: On the Theological Significance of the Hollow Point Network for the Doctrine of the Fate* — circulates through the priesthood faster than anything in his career. By the time the presentation comes, the Fate's central council has produced a position paper affirming that the migration is consistent with and expressive of the Fate's deepest doctrinal commitments.

You did not orchestrate this. You introduced Vann to the facts and stepped back. That's the job.

---

The Verath research. Three crates in a storage room at Cross's office. Forty years of expedition records, survey maps, First Empire textual analysis.

> *"The second phase,"* Cross says. *"The First Emperors."*
>
> *"Not yet. But the research needs to be preserved and available when it is."*

The scholars' quarter. The archive. Full attribution to Verath's specific researchers — not the house, the scholars who did the work. Their scholarship is separate from House Verath's conduct.

> *"I'll arrange it,"* Cross says.

---

## Part Seven: The Presentation

The Lord Commander's formal hall. Lords, merchant councils, the Fate's high priesthood, representatives from four coastal kingdoms.

Lady Ashe arrives the day before. She reads the title standing in the hallway.

> *"'In acknowledgment of the prior claim.' My daughter's name is Frontier. Did Crale tell you that?"*
>
> *"Yes."*
>
> *"The prior claim is real. The framework acknowledges it."* She reads Pen's summary. *"Who is Maret?"*
>
> *"A settler from Ashford. She stayed on the frontier after her village fell. She wrote that paragraph because she had been the person it describes."*
>
> *"She should be at the presentation."*
>
> *"She's in the hall."*

Lady Ashe completes her journey to support.

> *"All right. Let's go in."*

---

The presentation takes two days.

The first day goes well. The groundwork of three months has done what groundwork is supposed to do.

The second day: **Doven Marsh**, merchant council factor from Saltmere, asks the question no one has raised yet.

> *"The coastal cities. Thornwall, Saltmere, Greyvane. The migration addresses the plains settlers. It does not address the coastal city populations. The framework does not address what happens to them when the migration is complete."*

He is right.

You stand.

> *"You're right. The framework doesn't address the coastal cities. The framework was designed for the migration of the plains settlers to the mountain halls. The coastal cities are a second-order problem — not because they don't matter but because solving them requires the first problem to be solved first."*

You note that the mountain halls' capacity assumes the plains settlers only. The active expansion has no ceiling. Whether coastal city populations can eventually be included is a question for phase two.

The appendix gets a margin note, written in front of everyone: *coastal cities: phase two.*

Fourteen in favor. Three abstentions. Two opposed — both on the coastal city question, which means the opposition is not to the framework but to what the framework doesn't include.

*The Long Negotiation* is official.

---

## Epilogue: Root Rides North

The Lord Commander shakes your hand after the vote.

> *"The coastal cities."*
>
> *"Phase two. Marsh might do it. He identified the problem. People who identify problems clearly often have the architecture for solving them somewhere in the same thought."*
>
> *"You're going to write to Marsh,"* he says.
>
> *"Tomorrow,"* you say.

Root is at the reception. Ten thousand years of maintaining a working alone in a grove, standing in the Lord Commander's formal hall holding a cup in the human fashion.

> *"The working is stable. The pressure on the outer suppression is lower than it has been in three centuries. I estimate two years before I need to address the long-term sustainability question."*
>
> *"Will the other elves come south?"*
>
> *"I don't know. I need to go back. And bring them forward. And ask them to choose."*

A pause.

> *"I ride north tomorrow. Will you come with me to the grove's edge?"*
>
> *"Yes,"* you say. *"I'll come."*

Across the hall: Long Memory talking to the high priestess of the Fate. Maret talking to Lady Ashe with the directness that Maret brings to everything. The framework signed and official and beginning the long work of becoming real.

The winter wheat took.

You plant things.

You see if they take.

---

*End of Chapter Seven*
