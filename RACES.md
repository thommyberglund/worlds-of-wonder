# RACES
## Peoples of the World — Chronicle of the Central Plains

---

## Humans

**Distribution:** X=220–780, Y=500–850 (central plains and shattered coast; recolonizing north)
**Population:** Largest of any race; dominant on the central plains and coastal cities
**Lifespan:** 60–80 years
**Language:** The Common Tongue; regional dialects vary significantly between coastal cities and frontier settlements

### Overview

Humans are the youngest of the world's major peoples and the most numerous. They arrived from the east across a land bridge that has since sunk beneath the sea, bringing with them bronze tools, iron-working knowledge learned from the dwarfs, and a divine faith called the Fate — a belief in a single god whose power manifests through collective devotion rather than ritual magic.

In four thousand years humans transformed a single farming village on the central plains into the Empire of the Sun, the largest civilization the world has known in the current age. That empire is gone now, destroyed by the undead armies it accidentally awakened when its borders pushed south into the old ruins. What remains are the coastal cities — grand, fortified, and slowly mustering the will to reclaim what was lost.

Humans are defined less by biology than by circumstance. They are short-lived compared to elves and dwarfs, which makes them urgent and ambitious in ways those older peoples rarely understand. They build quickly, expand quickly, and lose quickly. The recolonization of the central plains is the defining project of the current age — a slow, costly, village-by-village reclamation of land that was humanity's heartland for millennia and is now a haunted wilderness.

### Physical Characteristics

Varied. Human populations across the coastal cities and frontier settlements show significant diversity in build, coloring, and stature — a consequence of multiple tribal waves arriving over thousands of years and mixing across a vast geography. The original Kwa-Hazalit bloodlines are traceable in some old coastal families but most humans are composites of a dozen tribal lineages.

Average height: 165–185cm. Average lifespan: 60–80 years, though frontier settlers frequently die younger.

### Culture and Society

Human society organizes around the Fate and around warfare. The two are not separate things. The priesthood of the Fate serves as both spiritual authority and administrative backbone of the coastal kingdoms, mediating between the feudal lords who control the plains territories and the merchant councils that run the harbor cities. Tension between these three powers — church, nobility, and commerce — defines most human political life.

The frontier is a different world. Villages like Ashford operate outside the reach of formal authority, governed by whoever has the most practical competence and the willingness to use it. The colonists who push back into the central plains are self-selected for hardiness and are treated by the coastal cities as a combination of valuable asset and acceptable loss.

### The Fate

The Fate is not magic in the scholarly sense. It does not manipulate the mechanics of the world the way wizard-craft does. It is collective belief made tangible — a city of devout followers praying at the moment of crisis can produce effects that no individual wizard could replicate. The sun-rite that ended the old empire and burned the undead from the plains for ten years was the single most powerful manifestation of the Fate in recorded history. It required the voluntary sacrifice of every believer within a thousand kilometers.

Priests of the Fate are not spellcasters. They are channels — the more people believe through them, the more the Fate's power flows. A solitary priest in a frontier village has modest gifts: blessings that hold in darkness, words that calm the frightened, light that keeps dead things at a distance. A high priest in a cathedral city at the center of a kingdom's faith is something considerably more.

## Humans — Vampire Subspecies

**Distribution:** Variable; concentrated in the deep south ruins and occasionally encountered on the frontier
**Population:** Unknown; self-limiting due to feeding requirements
**Lifespan:** Potentially immortal; functionally undying unless destroyed
**Language:** Common Tongue; older individuals may retain knowledge of imperial or pre-imperial languages

### Overview

Vampires are not the dead returned. They are the living transformed — humans altered at a fundamental level by exposure to the sorcerer-kings' blood-rite magic, which saturated the southern ruins for thousands of years before the Empire of the Sun pushed its borders into that territory.

The first vampires emerged during the Empire's southern expansion, when human necromancers began working with the eldritch residue of the First Empires and certain rites awakened the sorcerer-kings' closest minions. These minions required a living host to complete their transformation — a human body, altered by the rite, capable of receiving sustenance only from the blood of the living.

They are not mindless. This is the critical distinction between vampires and common undead. They reason, plan, remember, and suffer the awareness of what they have become. The oldest vampires — those that survived the sun-rite by retreating to deep underground chambers or the darkest ruins — carry memories of the Empire of the Sun at its height and before.

### Physical Characteristics

Superficially human. The transformation preserves the body in the state it occupied at the moment of turning, which means a vampire turned at sixty looks sixty forever and a vampire turned at twenty looks twenty until it is destroyed. Subtle signs accumulate over time: an unnatural stillness, eyes that catch and hold light in low conditions, skin that remains cool regardless of ambient temperature, and — most reliably — the sweetrot smell that intensifies with age and hunger.

They do not eat, drink, or breathe in the conventional sense, though old habits die slowly and some ancient vampires maintain the performance of these things for social reasons. They feed from living blood. Without it they do not die but enter a slow diminishment — increasingly still, increasingly cold, eventually achieving a torpor indistinguishable from death until blood is introduced.

### Weaknesses and Vulnerabilities

Sunlight is the primary vulnerability, a consequence of the sun-rite that nearly exterminated the species nine hundred years ago. Exposure to direct sunlight causes progressive and eventually total destruction. The duration varies by age — a newly turned vampire may survive brief exposure with severe injury while an ancient one may begin to combust within seconds. This is not a rule of nature so much as a scar left by the sun-rite itself, a resonance between the Fate's great working and the vampiric condition.

Fire, decapitation, and destruction of the heart are effective regardless of sunlight conditions. Holy symbols and the active power of the Fate cause pain and repulsion but are rarely immediately lethal — their effect depends almost entirely on the strength of belief behind them.

### Society and Organization

Vampires do not form communities in any conventional sense. Their feeding requirements make concentration impractical and their long memory makes trust difficult. What organization exists takes the form of hierarchies of age — older vampires command deference from younger ones through a combination of power, knowledge, and the simple advantage of having survived longer.

The oldest surviving vampires are believed to occupy the deepest ruins of the Silent Cities, in proximity to the entombed sorcerer-kings whose magic created them. Whether they serve those sleeping kings, guard them, or simply share their territory out of habit is unknown. They have not been encountered directly in the current age.

The vampires Aldric encountered on the frontier were of a young and feral variety — recently turned from humans during the undead surge, not yet possessed of the cunning and control that centuries develop. They are more dangerous to individuals but less dangerous to the world than the ancient ones sleeping in the south.

## Elves

**Distribution:** Island chains X=50–200, Y=500–700; scattered mainland groves X=250–450, Y=220–350
**Population:** Small and declining; largest concentrations on remote ocean islands
**Lifespan:** Functionally immortal; do not age after physical maturity
**Language:** The Elven tongue — considered by scholars to predate all other languages in the world and possibly to have arrived with the elves from wherever they originated

### Overview

The elves did not evolve on this world. This is not a theory or a legend — it is the considered position of every scholar who has studied their biology, their magic, and the persistent oral tradition of their own people. Legend says they arrived together with the three moons that hang in the night sky on cloudless evenings, which suggests their coming was not a migration but something more fundamental. They belong to this world now by tenure if not by birth, but the seam still shows.

Their magic is the magic of stasis — the prevention of change, the preservation of existing states, the denial of entropy. An elven grove does not age. The trees do not fall. The seasons slow at its borders. A grove tended by elves for ten thousand years looks as it did ten thousand years ago, because the elves will not permit it to look otherwise. This is not passive preservation. It is an active, continuous, and exhausting working of magical will against the natural tendency of all things to change.

This philosophy — stasis as the highest good, change as the fundamental enemy — defined elven civilization in its first age on this world. They were not merely preserving their groves. They were attempting to arrest the world itself, to hold the whole living system in a perfect unchanging moment. The original inhabitants of the plains and forests — the orcs and goblins — were obstacles to this project. The early elves eliminated them without particular malice, the way a gardener removes weeds.

Then came the civil war.

### The Civil War and Its Aftermath

The histories are fragmentary and the elves do not speak of it to outsiders. What can be assembled from ruins, from oblique references in dwarven records, and from the rare mainland elf willing to acknowledge that the subject exists, suggests that a significant faction within elven civilization came to regard the project of total stasis as monstrous — not only in its treatment of other peoples but in its fundamental nature. A world held perfectly still is not a world preserved. It is a world killed in a different way.

The civil war that followed was, by all accounts, the single most destructive conflict in the history of this world. The elven empire was not defeated by an external enemy. It destroyed itself. The static glades — the centers of elven power, where spirits were prepared for an eternity of perfect unchanging existence — were obliterated. The infrastructure of ten-thousand-year-old magical working was torn apart from within.

What survived were the elves who had hidden. Those who found deep forests and remote islands and made themselves small and careful and left the world mostly alone. These are the elves of the current age — the guarded ones, the fierce ones, the ones who protect their groves with a ferocity that looks, from outside, like the old mania but is actually its opposite. They are not trying to hold the world still anymore. They are trying to hold *themselves* still, to keep the remnant of their culture intact, to avoid becoming something they were once and choose not to be again.

### Physical Characteristics

Tall, averaging 180–195cm, with a physical quality that human observers consistently describe as *too precise* — features, movement, and proportion that suggest something engineered rather than evolved. Hair and eye coloring runs to pale extremes: silver, white, platinum, very dark black; grey, silver, and occasionally amber eyes. They do not age visibly after reaching physical maturity, which occurs slowly over their first century.

The immortality is not invulnerability. Elves die from violence, from disease, from despair. They simply do not die from time. The oldest living elves predate the current human civilization by thousands of years and carry in their memories a version of the world that no other living creature has seen.

### Magic

Elven magic is ambient and continuous rather than performed. A human wizard casts a spell — a discrete act with a beginning and an end. An elf *maintains* a state — a constant, background working that requires no conscious action but drains something over time that might be called vitality or presence. Elves who have maintained powerful workings for centuries become, in the observation of those few humans who have spent time near them, progressively less *present* — as though the magic is consuming the part of them that interfaces with the world.

The practical applications are significant. Elven-maintained groves resist disease, fire, and the incursion of undead with a passive reliability that no human fortification can match. This makes the mainland Sylvan Deep elves — the fierce ones in the boreal forests — accidental guardians of a territory that would otherwise be considerably more dangerous.

### The Elven Paradox

The elves' drive toward stasis may be the only thing slowing the natural cycles of the world enough to keep the sorcerer-kings asleep in their desert tombs. Life returning to the desert is the condition those ancient rulers are waiting for. Elven magic suppressing change across vast swaths of the northern world may be — inadvertently, ironically — the most important thing keeping the First Empires buried.

Or their magic may be exactly what the sorcerer-kings need to complete their own return. No one knows. The elves, if they have an opinion, are not sharing it.

## Dwarfs

**Distribution:** X=150–220, Y=200–700 (western mountains); X=780–850, Y=200–700 (eastern mountains)
**Population:** Moderate; stable for as long as records exist
**Lifespan:** 200–350 years
**Language:** Khazdul — a language of hard consonants and precise technical vocabulary; most dwarfs also speak the Common Tongue for trade

### Overview

The dwarfs are a mystery to themselves. Their elders, when asked about dwarven origins, state plainly that they do not know. There is no dwarven creation myth, no remembered moment of arrival, no founding story. They were in the mountains when the elves came, and they were in the mountains when the elves left, and they are in the mountains now. Whatever the mountains were before dwarfs occupied them, the dwarfs have no record of it.

This is not modesty or evasion. It appears to be genuine. Dwarven scholarship is meticulous and their oral tradition is maintained across centuries with extraordinary fidelity. If they had a memory of their origins, they would have kept it. The absence of that memory is itself significant — it suggests either that the origins predate memory in some fundamental way, or that something removed it.

What the dwarfs do have is craft. Their mountain kingdoms are marvels of engineering sustained over millennia — tunneled cities that expand generation by generation, deep mines that follow veins of iron and enchanted stone through the roots of the world, forges that have burned continuously for a thousand years. The iron and enchanted stone they export are the material backbone of human civilization. The coastal cities are built with dwarven metal. The armies that are mustering to retake the central plains will carry dwarven steel.

### Physical Characteristics

Compact and dense — dwarfs average 140–155cm in height but weigh as much as a tall human, the consequence of a skeletal density and muscle composition adapted to the pressures and close quarters of deep stone. Skin tones tend to grey-brown or reddish-brown, coloring suited to firelight and stone dust. Hair grows prolifically and is maintained with cultural significance — the specific braiding, cutting, and ornamentation of a dwarf's hair and beard communicates clan, profession, rank, and personal history to other dwarfs in a visual language that outsiders cannot easily read.

They see well in low light, hear well in confined spaces, and have an instinctive spatial awareness in underground environments that allows experienced dwarfs to navigate unmapped tunnels with a confidence that seems supernatural to human observers.

### Culture and Society

Dwarven society organizes around the clan and the craft. Every dwarf belongs to a clan — a lineage group that occupies a specific section of a mountain kingdom and specializes in a specific set of crafts or functions. Miners, smiths, engineers, soldiers, merchants — each profession has its associated clans, and the prestige of those clans rises and falls with the quality of their output and the shrewdness of their trade negotiations.

The mountain kingdoms are not unified. Each range operates as a collection of largely autonomous clan-halls connected by trade relationships, mutual defense agreements, and the shared infrastructure of the mountain itself. Inter-kingdom conflict is rare but inter-clan conflict within kingdoms is constant, contained by tradition and the practical reality that the mountains cannot sustain the destruction that surface warfare permits.

Dwarfs trade extensively with humans and have done so since the first human tribes arrived from the east. The relationship is pragmatic on both sides — humans need dwarven metal, dwarfs need human agricultural surplus and coastal access for external trade. Neither people particularly likes the other. Both people would be significantly worse off without the arrangement.

### Magic and Enchantment

Dwarfs do not practice magic in the elven or human wizard tradition. What they have is closer to applied understanding — a knowledge of the properties of certain stones, ores, and mineral combinations that, when worked at specific temperatures and with specific techniques, produce materials with properties that exceed the merely physical. Enchanted stone holds heat indefinitely. Certain dwarven alloys resist the touch of undead. Specific tunnel constructions dampen magical workings within them.

Whether this is magic or extremely sophisticated materials science is a debate that humans and dwarfs have conducted for four thousand years without resolution. The dwarfs find the question uninteresting. The materials work. That is sufficient.

## Orcs

**Distribution:** X=220–780, Y=501–750 (central plains); reclaiming territory abandoned by humans
**Population:** Increasing; significant presence in the abandoned frontier zones
**Lifespan:** 50–70 years
**Language:** Orcish — a language of few words and significant physical component; most orcs in contact with human settlements also speak a rough version of the Common Tongue

### Overview

Orcs are the original inhabitants of the central plains. They were here before the elves arrived, before the first human tribes crossed the land bridge, before any of the current world's great conflicts reshaped the geography of civilization. They have been displaced, slaughtered, pushed to the margins, and left for dead more times than their oral histories can count. They are still here.

The relationship between orcs and the rest of the world's peoples has been defined almost entirely by what others have done to them. The early elves slaughtered them casually during the colonization of the plains. The human empire pushed them steadily northward and eastward as farming expanded. When the empire fell and the central plains emptied, the orcs returned. They are one of the primary reasons the recolonization effort proceeds slowly — not because they are uniquely violent but because the land the colonists are reclaiming is land the orcs have been reoccupying for eight hundred years and do not intend to vacate.

This is typically framed, in human accounts, as orc aggression. In orcish accounts — to the extent that outsiders have access to them — it is framed as the only rational response to a people who have tried to remove them from their ancestral territory multiple times across multiple millennia.

### Physical Characteristics

Large and powerful, averaging 185–205cm with a muscular build that reflects a people who have survived by physical capability when political and military organization were unavailable to them. Skin tones run from grey-green to deep olive. Prominent lower canines — not tusks in the popular imagination but enlarged canine teeth, functional rather than ornamental. Eyes typically amber or dark brown, well-adapted to low light.

Orcs mature faster than humans — physically adult by fifteen, considered elder by fifty. The short lifespan is partly biology and partly the accumulated effect of living in contested territory without the protections that city walls and standing armies provide.

### Culture and Society

Orcish society on the reclaimed central plains organizes around the warband — a mobile group of ten to sixty individuals bound by kinship, choice, or mutual survival interest, led by whoever has demonstrated the combination of strength, cunning, and judgment that the group accepts as sufficient. Leadership is not hereditary. It is demonstrated continuously. A warchief who makes bad decisions loses followers until they cease to be a warchief.

The warbands are not mindless raiders. They farm, hunt, trade among themselves, and maintain a complex web of territorial relationships across the plains. The conflict with human colonists is genuine and frequently violent, but it coexists with pragmatic trade where proximity and mutual need make it sensible. Frontier villages that establish working relationships with local warbands survive longer than those that treat every orc sighting as a military threat.

The deeper orcish culture — the traditions, stories, and spiritual practices that predate the elven arrival — is largely intact within the warbands, passed through oral tradition maintained by figures who occupy a role something between elder, historian, and priest. These figures are called the Rememberers. They do not fight. Their function is considered too important to risk in battle. Whatever they remember is considered more valuable than anything a warrior could accomplish.

What they remember, in part, is what the world looked like before anyone else arrived. This knowledge is not shared with outsiders.

### Orcs and the Undead

The orcish oral tradition contains the most detailed non-human account of the undead surge that destroyed the Empire of the Sun. The Rememberers' version differs from the human account in significant ways — primarily in attributing the surge not to random necromantic activity but to a specific sequence of events at specific locations that the human empire triggered through its southern expansion.

Whether this account is accurate, embellished, or a retelling of something the Rememberers only partially understood at the time is unknown. The dwarfs, who also have records from the period, have not commented publicly.

## Goblins

**Distribution:** X=220–780, Y=501–750 (central plains, forests, and cave systems); found in most environments that humans and orcs occupy
**Population:** Large; difficult to census accurately due to dispersal and rapid reproduction
**Lifespan:** 30–45 years
**Language:** Goblin — fast, complex, heavily contextual, and extremely difficult for other species to learn; most goblins in contact with human settlements develop functional Common Tongue quickly

### Overview

Goblins are, alongside orcs, the original inhabitants of the world's plains and forests. They are also, in the current age, among the most numerous living peoples — a fact that their size and the tendency of other species to underestimate them has obscured in most historical accounts.

They are small. They are fast. They breed quickly, mature quickly, and die quickly by the standards of longer-lived races. They adapt to new environments and changing conditions with a speed that elves find incomprehensible and dwarfs grudgingly admire. Where orcs reclaim territory by force of arms, goblins reclaim it by simply being present in large numbers in places that other species have vacated or failed to control.

The central plains, the frontier forests, and the cave systems beneath both are full of goblins. The abandoned villages of the recolonization zone that humans have not yet reached, or have retreated from, are frequently occupied by goblin clans within a season of human departure. They do not build in the human manner — they adapt existing structures, adding to them in ways that look chaotic from outside but reflect an internal logic of function over form.

### Physical Characteristics

Small — 100–130cm, lightly built, weighing roughly half what a human of similar height would weigh. Skin tones run from yellow-green to grey-brown, typically matching the dominant substrate of their home environment with a looseness that suggests some adaptive capacity. Large eyes well-suited to low light and rapid movement tracking. Ears that move independently and provide exceptional directional hearing. Hands with an extra joint in each finger that gives a grip strength disproportionate to their overall size.

The short lifespan is the defining physical fact of goblin existence. A goblin is considered old at thirty-five. The urgency this creates — the speed of decision-making, the willingness to take risks that longer-lived species would calculate more carefully, the enormous value placed on experience because there is so little time to accumulate it — shapes goblin culture at every level.

### Culture and Society

Goblin society organizes around the clan, which is simultaneously a kinship group, an economic unit, and a military formation. Clans range from a dozen to several hundred individuals. Multiple clans in the same territory form loose confederations for mutual defense and resource sharing, with the understanding that the confederation dissolves the moment cooperation becomes more costly than independence.

Goblins are not, in the main, aggressive toward humans or orcs without provocation — a persistent human misconception rooted partly in the raiding behavior of goblin clans under resource pressure and partly in the historical habit of treating goblin territorial occupation as inherently illegitimate. A goblin clan that has occupied an abandoned human farmstead does not consider itself to be trespassing. The humans who return to reclaim it disagree. The resulting violence gets recorded as goblin aggression.

The internal life of goblin clans — their art, their music, their elaborate system of obligation and debt-tracking, their relationship with the cave systems that many clans use as refuges and that some clans treat as sacred — is almost entirely undocumented in human scholarship. The goblins have not complained about this. They appear to prefer it.

### Goblins and the Underground

Some goblin clans extend their territory deep into cave systems, following routes that descend into parts of the underground that other species have not mapped. What these deep clans encounter — and whether their relationship with the Necromantic Hollows, the ancient catacombs that underlie the plains, is one of avoidance or engagement — is a question that scholars have been unable to answer, partly because the goblins who know the answer are not talking and partly because the scholars willing to follow them underground tend not to return with useful notes.
