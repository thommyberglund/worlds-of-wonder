# World Summary
## Chronicle of the Central Plains — Setting Overview

---

## The Shape of the World

The geography runs from frozen polar wastes in the far north through dark boreal forests, down to temperate central plains, and finally to hot southern jungles and deserts. Two great mountain ranges flank the plains on east and west. The warm sea sits at the southern edge of the civilized coast. Beyond that, jungle and desert stretch toward ruins that most people prefer not to think about.

---

## What Happened

Human civilization peaked and collapsed. The Empire of the Sun spent four thousand years building the largest human civilization the world had known, then pushed its southern borders into ruins it didn't understand. What came out of those ruins destroyed everything. Vampires. Undead armies. Eventually the last surviving priests of the human faith performed a rite that may have consumed the souls of every believer within a thousand kilometres — and it worked. Ten years of eternal daylight burned the undead from the plains. When night finally returned, not a single human remained between the mountains and the sea.

That was eight hundred years ago.

---

## The Present

Humanity clings to fortified coastal cities — grand, wealthy, and heavily walled — and is only now beginning the slow, costly work of reclaiming the heartland. Frontier villages push north into the central plains one settlement at a time. They fight orcs during the day and undead during the night, and the nearest help is usually several hours away. The coastal cities send supplies and settlers but treat the colonists as a combination of valuable asset and acceptable loss.

The central plains are haunted. The old imperial capital stands empty in the middle of them, its boulevards slowly filling with grass, its streets still walked by the dead. Most of what the empire built is gone or reclaimed by wilderness.

---

## Who Shares the World

**Orcs and goblins** were here before humans arrived and are here still. The orcs reclaimed large portions of the plains during the eight centuries of human absence and have no intention of leaving. They are not monsters — they are a people with long memories and legitimate grievances, and the recolonisation effort looks from their perspective like the third or fourth attempt to remove them from their ancestral land. Goblins are everywhere humans aren't, filling abandoned villages and cave systems with a speed and adaptability that longer-lived races find difficult to comprehend.

**Elves** are few, ancient, and deliberately absent. Most withdrew to remote ocean islands after a civil war destroyed their empire millennia ago. The mainland survivors guard deep forest groves with a ferocity that keeps the boreal belt safer than it has any right to be — possibly inadvertently, possibly not.

**Dwarfs** hold their mountain kingdoms in both ranges and have traded with humans for four thousand years. The relationship is pragmatic and mutually dependent rather than warm.

---

## What Waits Underneath

In the deep south, beneath desert sands, the original rulers of this world are entombed and waiting. The sorcerer-kings of the First Empires drained their land of all life to fuel blood rites that granted them immortality, then chose entombment when there was nothing left to consume. Their condition for waking is simple: the desert must bloom again. Life returning to the south is the dinner bell. Eight centuries of human absence from the plains means the wilderness is slowly recovering. Nobody has said this out loud yet, but the math is not comforting.

---

## The Tone

The world doesn't care whether anyone survives. Magic is a wound, not a gift. The gods are real but capricious and expensive. The dead don't stay buried. Heroes are people who haven't died in the mud yet — and the mud is everywhere.
